# Forecast ahead from a regime-switching (HMM) fit

Draws from the posterior predictive distribution `h` steps beyond the
end of the series, integrating over both parameter uncertainty (one path
per posterior draw) and regime uncertainty (the regime is sampled, not
fixed).

## Usage

``` r
nimixForecast(
  object,
  h = 1L,
  newdata = NULL,
  lags = NULL,
  draws = 500L,
  level = 0.9
)
```

## Arguments

- object:

  A
  [`FitResult`](https://madsyair.github.io/nimix/reference/FitResult-class.md)
  fitted with `method = "hmm"`.

- h:

  Integer horizon (\>= 1).

- newdata:

  For a Markov-switching regression, a data frame of `h` future rows
  supplying the *exogenous* predictors. Ignored otherwise.

- lags:

  Named integer vector marking predictors that are the response in
  disguise, e.g. `lags = c(ylag = 1)` for a column holding \\y\_{t-1}\\.
  Those columns are **generated by the forecast**, not supplied to it:
  each posterior draw feeds its own trajectory back as its own lag, so
  the uncertainty compounds the way an autoregression's should. Give a
  predictor in `lags` or in `newdata`, never both.

- draws:

  Maximum posterior draws to use (thinned evenly).

- level:

  Central credible level for the interval.

## Value

A list with `summary` (a data frame of `h` rows: `mean`, `median`,
`lower`, `upper`), `regime` (an `h` x `K` matrix of regime
probabilities), and `draws` (the raw `draws` x `h` predictive sample).

## Details

The recipe is the forward algorithm run one step further: filter the
regime distribution to the last observation, push it through the
transition matrix \\h\\ times, sample a regime from it, and draw from
that regime's emission.

## What an HMM can and cannot forecast

The point forecast decays toward the stationary mixture as `h` grows, at
rate \\\|\lambda_2\|^h\\ in the second eigenvalue of the transition
matrix. That is the model working, not failing: an HMM forecasts the
*regime*, and once the regime is unknowable the best guess for `y` is
the long-run average over regimes. Measured on a two-regime series at
+/-2 with persistence 0.95/0.90, the median forecast fell from 1.8 at
`h = 1` to -1.1 by `h = 12`, while the regime probabilities went from
0.11/0.89 to 0.59/0.41 – converging on the stationary 0.67/0.33.
Reversion, not signal. Interval coverage over the same horizon was 0.917
against a nominal 0.90.

**Everything hinges on whether the regime persists.** On a
Markov-switching regression whose regime held across the forecast
boundary, the median forecast beat a constant-mean benchmark fivefold
(RMSE 0.50 against 2.65); on a series that happened to switch at exactly
the first forecast step – a 5% event under the fitted persistence – it
lost (4.42 against 2.45). That is not a defect to tune away: a switch
one step ahead is unpredictable by construction, and the model correctly
assigned it low probability beforehand. The interval still covered.

**Beware the point summary when regimes disagree.** With well separated
regimes the predictive is *bimodal*, and its median sits in the trough
between the modes – where the model puts almost no mass. `$summary` is
convenient, not faithful; `$draws` is the honest object, and `$regime`
usually answers the question actually being asked ("which regime will we
be in"), degrading far more gracefully than any forecast of `y`.

For a trend or a seasonal pattern this is the wrong model; nimix does
not fit those.

## Autoregression

A Markov-switching autoregression is an ordinary Markov-switching
regression with the response lagged into the design matrix
(`nimixReg(y ~ ylag, df, K = 2, method = "hmm")`), and forecasting one
needs `lags` rather than `newdata`: nobody knows the future \\y\\, so
the lag has to come from the forecast itself. Each posterior draw
carries its own trajectory and feeds it back as its own lag, which is
what makes the interval widen properly – measured on an MS-AR(1), from
2.15 at `h = 1` to 5.12 at `h = 10`, with RMSE 0.54 against 3.97 for a
constant-mean benchmark. With exogenous predictors as well, pass those
in `newdata` and the lags in `lags` (measured on an MS-ARX: RMSE 0.83
against 5.29).

**Do not fake a lag through `newdata`.** It will be taken as a known
covariate, held fixed, and the forecast will inherit whatever you
invented – confidently and without complaint. `lags` exists so that you
do not have to.

## Examples

``` r
if (FALSE) { # \dontrun{
f  <- nimixClust(y, K = 2, method = "hmm")
fc <- nimixForecast(f, h = 6)
fc$summary
fc$regime          # usually the more informative half

# a Markov-switching AR(1): the lag is generated, not supplied
g  <- nimixReg(y ~ ylag, df, K = 2, method = "hmm")
nimixForecast(g, h = 10, lags = c(ylag = 1))
} # }
```
